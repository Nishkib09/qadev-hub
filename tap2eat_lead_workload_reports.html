Scenario A
You have a Quality Assessor in post
The QA handles daily monitoring, scoring, and coaching logs. Your job is to lead the system — design the standards, manage the assessor, run training, analyse the data, and own the outcomes. You are the architect, not the bricklayer.

30m

Review yesterday's QA dashboard summary submitted by your assessor. Confirm evaluation targets for today — which agents, which channels, any priority call types. Quick 10-min stand-up with the QA Assessor: what's in the queue, any carry-over from yesterday, any risks to flag. You're not picking up a headset — you're setting the day's direction.

60m

Use the protected morning block to build or update training content. Today: revising the escalation scenarios module based on last week's QA data showing 3 agents escalated unnecessarily. Create 2 new scenario cards with debrief notes. This is strategic work that the QA frees you to do — designing better systems, not just measuring the current one.

No QA in post — you are carrying both roles
Every evaluation, every coaching log, every calibration, every training session, every report, every intern supervision — all of it lands on one person. This is not sustainable indefinitely. This document shows the reality clearly so that the workload is visible, not invisible.

30m

You arrive 30 minutes before the floor opens. Load the recording system and score 3 call recordings from yesterday's afternoon shift before agents arrive. This is the only guaranteed quiet evaluation window. Without a QA, every evaluation has to be squeezed around everything else — you start early to protect some of the scoring time.

No QA to brief. Instead you do a direct check-in with the Team Lead on the floor. Any incidents from yesterday? Any agent concerns? Confirm the evaluation queue for today is realistic given everything else in the diary. Flag immediately if there are interns in nesting — their supervision cannot be deprioritised.
🎯 Floor Leadership👥 Team Check-In

Score 4 interactions — calls and/or WhatsApp — from the current active agents. Complete the scorecard for each. Annotate coaching notes as you go because there's no QA to hand these to — you need them ready for later coaching sessions. This block alone would be the QA Assessor's primary morning task. You're doing it on top of everything else.
🔍 QA Scoring⚠️ QA Role Cover
⚠️Strategic training development work that would normally happen in this block is postponed. Content creation and SOP building shifts to evenings or weekends unless protected time is formally allocated.
9:00
30m
Coaching Session — Agent From Yesterday's Low Score
You scored the call, you wrote the coaching notes, and now you're delivering the coaching session too. Pull up the recording, walk through the scorecard, discuss the specific moments. Sign the coaching log. In a normal structure, the QA Assessor handles first-pass coaching; you handle complex or escalated cases. Right now there is no first-pass filter — every coaching conversation comes to you.

Deliver Module 2 to the intern group. You cannot skip this or delegate it — training is your core function and these interns go into nesting in 3 days. The challenge: you just came from scoring calls and running a coaching session. Focus is required. Energy management on a day like this is a skill in itself. Deliver fully, note comprehension gaps, book follow-ups.
📚 Training Delivery
⚠️You cannot do live floor monitoring during this session. Any real-time quality failures on the floor while you're in training are invisible until you review recordings later.

Sit in on 2 supervised live calls with the nesting intern. Score them. Give immediate verbal feedback. Log the nesting scores. This is non-negotiable — no intern goes unsupervised in the nesting period. But without a QA, you are the only person who can do this, meaning every other task must pause when a nesting call is live.

💡 The Business Case These Comparisons Build
What this comparison proves
The gap between the two columns is not a personal capacity gap — it is a structural resourcing gap. The Q&T Lead role was designed to function with a QA Assessor in place. Operating without one means one person is delivering the outputs of two roles, which compresses strategic work, limits evaluation coverage, and creates quality blind spots that compound over time.
The Risk of Silence
If this dual-role situation is not documented and reported, it becomes the new normal. Leadership assumes the quality function is operating at full capacity. It is not. The weekly and termly reports in the next two tabs exist partly to make this visible — factually, not as a complaint.
